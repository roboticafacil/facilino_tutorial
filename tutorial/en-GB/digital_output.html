An Arduino's digital outputs detailed description

Digital Signals

Digital signals can only take two possible values HIGH or LOW. We refer to LOW when the voltage of a signal is closed to 0V, while HIGH implies that the voltage of the signal is close to 5V (for Arduino Uno).

(From Wikipedia) A digital signal is a signal that is being used to represent data as a sequence of discrete values; at any given time it can only take on one of a finite number of values.
Digital Outputs

A digital output can only have two possible states LOW or HIGH. When setting a digital output to a LOW state, the voltage at its corresponding pin will be 0V, while setting the output to a HIGH state will make the digital pin to have a voltage of 5V.

In the following example, when we set the Digital Output to a HIGH state will make the pin to have 5V, the current (arrow in black) flows through resistor R2 and the diode between the Q1 transistor base (B) and emitter (E). This causes transistor Q1 to behave in shortcircuit mode between colector (C) and emitter (E). As a consequence, there's a current flow through R1 which makes the LED to light on. As the voltage in C is nearly 0V, the resistor R1 will hold approximately 4.3V, considering that the typical voltage voltage drop in a diode is 0.7V. If the digital output is set to a LOW state, there will be no current flow through the B-E diode in Q1, which will make the transistor to be in opercircuit mode. Therefore, the won't be any current flowing through R1, which in turns will make the LED to switch off.
