Digital Signals

Binary digital signals can only take two possible values HIGH or LOW. We refer to LOW when the voltage of a signal is closed to 0V, while HIGH implies that the voltage of the signal is close to 5V (for Arduino Uno).

(From Wikipedia) A digital signal is a signal that is being used to represent data as a sequence of discrete values; at any given time it can only take on one of a finite number of values.
Digital Inputs

A digital input is usually understood from Arduino's point of view as the type of signal such that a sensor sets the voltage value of the signal to 5V or 0V and it is interpreted as HIGH or LOW. Some sensors only provide 3.3V, which is also compatible with Arduino and its read as HIGH.

In the following example, when the switch SW1 is closed, the voltage measured in Arduino's "Digital input" pin is 0V, while if the switch is open, then the voltage value measured is 5V (using an internal pull-up resistor).

When the switch is closed the current will flow through resistor R1, because the switch forces to be one of the resistor ends to be 0V and the voltage drop at the resistor will be 5V. The current will be about 0.5mA, because  5V/10KOhms=0.5mA. The current flow sourcing from the "Digital input" is very small.

When the switch is open there's a very small current flow sinking to the digital input. The voltage at the "Digital Input" pin is clearly 5V, because of the small value of the current.
