Buzzer

A Buzzer, or also know as a Beeper is an audio singalling device. Typical uses include notifications, alarms or confirmation devices.

A Passive Buzzer has a pezoelectric layer, that makes the membrane vibrate when a current is applied to it. If a PWM signal is applied, the frequency of such signal is converted into a sound with a vibration of that frequency. We can generate notes at indicated frequency and duration.

One of the most common modules is the KY-006, a passive buzzer with three pins, one for ground, one for 5V voltage supply and on to receive a PWM digital signal. Connect them to the desired digital pin and start making some sound.

The Buzzer can also create different notes, that can be combined to create melodies. You can select the desire frequency or corresponding note inside the Facilino block instruction. Be aware of the duration and pauses you want be played.
Coding Basic Sounds with Facilino

Facilino provides several instructions simple sounds and notes using the buzzer. The first instruction allows you to generate notes based on their names (you can select the duration and decide whether or not to tie it with the next one. The second instruction is used to reproduced predefined sounds, while the third instruction allows you to generate advanced sounds combining some parameters. The fourth instruction will stop reproducing sound on the selected pin and thus can be used for other purposes onwards.

Coding Melodies with Facilino

Facilino provides also instructions to reproduce melodies. Melodies are indeed a sequence of frequencies and durations of notes. The first instruction is used to reproduce melodies. When the background task option is selected, the instruction does not wait upon completion (it reproduces the melody on a background task to allow you execute other instructions in parallel. The second instruction provides some predefined melodies, that can be used in robotic projects, for instance. The third instruction stops the melody if it is being executed in the background task, while the fourth instruction checks whether or not the melody is still being played. The fifth and sixth instructions are two representative instruction to create custom melodies. You can add as many notes and silences similars to the fifth instruction, but make sure you add the sixth instruction at the end.
