Electronics Semiconductors

The fundamental concepts on Semiconductors

Semiconductors

Semiconductors are an fundamental components in complex electronics. You find them at the heart of every Proccessing element (example. microchips), anything that's computerized or uses radio waves depends on semiconductors.

They get their name from having an electrical conductivity value falling between that of a conductor and an insulator. Its importance come from the fact that their conduction properties may be influenced by external factors such as electric potential or temperature. They are mostly non linear and non passive.

Today, most semiconductor chips and transistors are created with silicon.

Diodes

Diodes can be used as rectifiers, signal limiters, voltage regulators, switches, signal modulators, signal mixers, signal demodulators, and oscillators. 

Unlike a resistor, a diode does not behave linearly with respect to the applied voltage as it has an exponential I-V relationship and therefore can not be described simply by using Ohm’s law. The fundamental property of a diode is its tendency to conduct electric current in only one direction. 

The most known diodes are LEDs which stand for Light Emitting Diodes, while conducting they convert some of the dropped volatge into Light.

Transistors
.

A Transistor is a semiconductor device used to amplify or switch electronic signals and electrical power. The fundamental property of a Transistor is its tendency to conduct electric current based on a electric potential of a external terminal. 

This is probably the hardest of the basic electronic components to understand. But don’t worry, it’s not that hard.

There are two types of basic transistor out there: bi-polar junction (BJT) and metal-oxide field-effect (MOSFET). Both have further two types of Setups; NPN and PNP. For now we will focus on the BJT NPN Transistor.

They are three-terminal devices. On a bi-polar junction transistor (BJT), those pins are labeled Collector (C), Base (B), and Emitter (E).

A simple way is to look at the transistor as a switch controlled by an electrical signal.If you put a certain voltage between the base and the emitter, you turn it on.

But, instead of having just two states (ON or OFF), it can also be “a bit on” by controlling the current that goes through its base.

A bit of current on the base produces a current of maybe 100 times more (depending on the transistor) through the Collector and Emitter. 

Thrystors

A Thyristor, also known as a SCR (silicon controlled rectifier), is a special type of diode that only allows current to flow when a control voltage is applied to it's gate terminal. But once activated it will continue to conduct until no more electricity flows forward.

It is in the relative timing of these two functions that the thyristor has it's most important role, for example in Power Control. When the thyristor is activated, it will start to drive and stop doing so when the current (is null). In the case of a resistive circuit, the current will be canceled when the voltage of the alternating current signal is canceled, that is, it passes through zero (or below the holding current). This generates a 'cutting' effect of the original wave that allows regulating the effective power.

A triac is like a thyristor, but current can flow in both directions. It is as if two opposed thyristors with shared gate pin.
